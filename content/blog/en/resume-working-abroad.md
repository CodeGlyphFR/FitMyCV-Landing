## Why Your Local Resume Won't Work Overseas

You found a great job listing in Berlin, Toronto, or Barcelona. You translate your resume, hit send... and hear nothing back. This experience is frustratingly common. The reason? A word-for-word translation of your resume almost never matches the expectations of a foreign job market.

Every country has its own conventions for job applications. Resume length, personal details, section structure, tone, and formatting all vary from one market to the next. A German recruiter expects something very different from an American one, and a resume that works in Australia may fall flat in France.

## Key Resume Differences by Country

### The American Resume

The American resume is known for its brevity and results-driven focus. It typically runs one page (two maximum for senior professionals) and prioritizes impact over description.

**Key points:**
- No photo, no date of birth, no marital status (anti-discrimination laws)
- A strong "Summary" or "Professional Profile" at the top
- Bullet points with quantified achievements
- Clear separation between hard skills and soft skills
- No mention of nationality or visa status on the resume itself

**Example of an effective bullet point:**
"Increased quarterly revenue by 23% through implementation of targeted B2B marketing campaigns" rather than "Responsible for marketing campaigns."

### The British CV

The British CV shares similarities with the American resume but has its own nuances.

**Key points:**
- Two pages are acceptable and even expected for experienced professionals
- A "Personal Statement" at the top replaces the American "Summary"
- References are typically not included (just "Available upon request")
- Postgraduate qualifications are highly valued
- UK-specific terminology matters (e.g., "CV" not "resume," "holiday" not "vacation")

### The German Lebenslauf

Germany is known for its rigorous application conventions. The Lebenslauf tends to be more structured and formal.

**Key points:**
- Professional photo almost always expected
- Tabular format (dates on the left, descriptions on the right)
- Reverse chronological order
- Date of birth and nationality commonly included
- Certifications and continuing education are highly valued
- A "Bewerbungsfoto" (application photo) should meet professional standards

### The Spanish CV

In Spain and Latin America, conventions differ significantly.

**Key points:**
- Photo generally included
- More detailed personal information (date of birth, nationality, sometimes full address)
- The Europass format is still common for EU applications
- Education ("Formación") section often given significant space
- Internships and volunteer work are valued, especially for recent graduates

## Common Mistakes When Adapting Your Resume for Abroad

### 1. Translating Literally Instead of Adapting

Word-for-word translation is the number one trap. Job titles, degrees, and skills don't always have direct equivalents. A French "DUT Techniques de commercialisation" means nothing to an American recruiter. You need the local equivalent: "Two-Year University Diploma in Marketing" or "Associate Degree in Marketing."

### 2. Keeping Your Home Country's Structure

If your resume follows a format typical of your country (e.g., a two-column French CV with a "Hobbies" section), it may seem out of place in other markets. Research and adopt the structure expected in your target country.

### 3. Forgetting Local Keywords

Every market has its own professional jargon. "Project management" in English, "Projektleitung" in German, "Gestión de proyectos" in Spanish. [ATS systems](/blog/ats-resume-complete-guide) used by international companies filter on these keywords in the local language.

### 4. Ignoring Format Conventions

Sending a three-page resume to an American recruiter, including a photo for a UK application, or forgetting the photo for a German position: these format mistakes signal a lack of familiarity with the local market.

### 5. Overlooking International ATS Requirements

Large international companies use [ATS systems that filter applications](/blog/ats-resume-mistakes) in every country. Your resume needs to be both culturally adapted and [compatible with these automated systems](/blog/ats-compatible-resume-format).

## How to Effectively Prepare an International Resume

### Step 1: Research Your Target Market

Before changing anything, learn the local conventions. Browse job listings in your field on the target country's platforms (local LinkedIn, Indeed, StepStone for Germany, InfoJobs for Spain). Pay attention to job titles, vocabulary, and skills highlighted.

### Step 2: Rephrase, Don't Translate

Every bullet point in your resume should be rephrased to match local expectations. This means adapting vocabulary, tone, and sentence structure. Quantified results are universally appreciated, but how you present them varies.

### Step 3: Adapt Your Qualifications

Educational systems differ radically from country to country. Always include the original name of your degree followed by a locally understandable equivalent. For example: "Licence en Informatique (equivalent to BSc in Computer Science)."

### Step 4: Highlight Your Language Skills

Working abroad means communicating in another language. Your [language skills](/blog/multilingual-resume-language-skills) become a major asset. Use the CEFR framework (A1-C2) for European markets, or clear descriptors like "Professional working proficiency" for North American markets.

### Step 5: Optimize for International ATS

A [match score](/blog/resume-match-score-explained) lets you verify that your resume aligns with the target job's requirements, including keywords. This is especially important when applying in a language that isn't your native one.
