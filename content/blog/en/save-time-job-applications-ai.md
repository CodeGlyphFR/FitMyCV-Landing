## The Problem: Job Hunting Is a Full-Time Job

Looking for a job is, paradoxically, one of the most time-consuming activities that exists. According to a recent study, an active job seeker spends an average of 11 hours per week on their job search. The breakdown is revealing:

- 3 to 4 hours browsing job postings
- 4 to 5 hours tailoring resumes and writing cover letters
- 2 to 3 hours on administrative tasks (tracking, follow-ups, interview prep)

For someone already employed and searching discreetly, those 11 hours have to be squeezed into evenings and weekends. For a full-time job seeker, this pace quickly becomes exhausting and demoralizing, especially when responses are slow to come.

## Where Do You Lose the Most Time?

### Resume Tailoring: The Bottleneck

The most time-consuming task is adapting your resume to each posting. A conscientious candidate who customizes their resume for every application spends between 30 and 60 minutes per posting. For 10 applications per week, that's 5 to 10 hours dedicated solely to this task.

This time breaks down as follows:
- **Reading and analyzing the posting** (10 min): identifying key skills, vocabulary, requirements
- **Rephrasing experiences** (15-20 min): adapting descriptions to match the posting
- **Adjusting skills** (5-10 min): reordering and completing the list
- **Review and formatting** (10-15 min): proofreading, layout, export

To understand why this customization remains essential, read our article on [why you should customize your resume for every posting](/blog/customize-resume-every-job-posting).

### Repeating Identical Tasks

The worst part of this process is the repetition. For each application, you essentially do the same thing: read a posting, compare with your profile, adjust the resume. The actions are the same, only the content changes. This is exactly the type of task that automation can optimize.

## Strategies to Maximize Efficiency

### Batch Your Application Sessions

Instead of applying to one posting at a time throughout the day, batch your applications into sessions. For example:
- Morning session (30 min): browse new postings and launch AI analyses
- Evening session (30 min): review generated resumes, validate and apply

This batch approach is more efficient than processing postings one at a time.

### Create Variants of Your Base Resume

If you're applying across different fields, create 2 or 3 variants of your base resume. A management-oriented version, a technical version, a commercial version. AI will start from the closest variant for each posting, which further reduces the number of modifications needed.

### Prioritize with the Match Score

Don't spend the same amount of time on every application. Use the match score to prioritize:
- **Score > 80%**: quick application, few modifications needed
- **Score 60-80%**: invest a bit more time in customization
- **Score < 50%**: evaluate whether the application is truly worth the effort before investing time

To better understand score thresholds, read our article on the [match score](/blog/resume-match-score-explained).

### Save Your Best Phrasings

Some rephrasings proposed by AI are particularly effective. Note them for reuse in future applications. Over time, you'll build a library of effective phrasings that will further accelerate the process.
